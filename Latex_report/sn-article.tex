\title[Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble for EV Charging Forecast]{Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble: A Multi-Horizon Architecture for EV Charging Utilization Forecasting}

\author*[1, 2]{\fnm{Xuan-Thanh} \sur{Trinh}}\email{thanh.tx20222744@hust.edu.vn}
\author[1, 2]{\fnm{Trung-Hai} \sur{Hoang}}
\author[1, 2]{\fnm{Quang-Anh} \sur{Nguyen}}
\author[1, 2]{\fnm{Ba-Minh-Dang} \sur{Vu}}

\affil*[1]{\orgname{School of Electrical and Electronic Engineering}}
\affil[2]{\orgname{Hanoi University of Science and Technology}, \orgaddress{\city{Hanoi}, \country{Vietnam}}}

\abstract{The rapid expansion of Electric Vehicle (EV) charging infrastructure introduces severe non-linear fluctuations in grid demand, necessitating highly accurate utilization forecasting models. Traditional forecasting methods often fail to capture abrupt load spikes or suffer from phase lag when attempting multi-horizon predictions. In this paper, we propose a novel Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble architecture inspired by Model Predictive Control (MPC) principles. The architecture consists of a 24-hour Rule-Based Two-Stage trajectory model and a 1-hour Micro-Tuner model. By employing Target Power Transformation (TPT) alongside a mathematically formulated Custom Peak Loss function, our methodology effectively captures abrupt load spikes while strictly avoiding data leakage. We demonstrate that tree-based ensemble models, specifically XGBoost, achieve a state-of-the-art RMSE of 0.0664, surpassing both deep learning approaches and traditional time-series baselines. The robust theoretical alignment between the macro-level predictive trajectory and micro-level agile correction establishes a new standard for EV demand forecasting, ensuring stability for modern smart grids.}

\keywords{Electric Vehicle, Load Forecasting, XGBoost, Proxy Feature Boosting, Phase Lag, Two-Stage Architecture}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec1}

The rapid proliferation of electric vehicles (EVs) introduces highly stochastic and non-linear bimodal peak patterns into the electrical grid, rendering accurate utilization forecasting a critical necessity. However, a fundamental challenge persists in modern forecasting approaches: the pursuit of high accuracy often leads to deploying data-intensive deep neural networks \cite{eren2024comprehensive, zhu2019electric}, yet real-world station-level EV data remains characteristically sparse. While monolithic deep learning architectures excel under massive data regimes, they suffer from severe overfitting and computational overhead when confronted with limited sample sizes. Conversely, ensemble tree-based models, such as XGBoost and Random Forest, have demonstrated superior generalization capabilities and robustness in small-data environments \cite{lu2021comparative, wang2020ensemble, shahriar2020machine}. This establishes a compelling imperative to pivot from complex neural networks to efficient tabular algorithms as the foundational block for EV load prediction.

Despite the algorithmic advantages of tree-based models, standard regression techniques inherently optimize for mean-seeking metrics like Mean Squared Error (MSE), leading to systematic underestimation of extreme peaks. To address this, many researchers resort to input-space manipulation techniques like SMOGN to oversample minority peak data. However, such synthetic augmentations irreparably distort the underlying distribution of categorical features, compromising the physical interpretability of the data. Recent advancements strongly advocate for output-space interventions instead, demonstrating that asymmetric and custom loss functions \cite{wu2021support, ko2025deterministic, kim2026methodology} paired with domain knowledge can heavily penalize under-forecasting without compromising data integrity. This validates the shift toward Target Power Transformation (TPT) and custom loss functions, yet extreme peak-catching mathematically compromises the accuracy at off-peak valleys, revealing that a single monolithic model cannot simultaneously master both extremes.

The intrinsic trade-off between peak sensitivity and valley stability necessitates a structural divide-and-conquer approach. Traditional methodologies often introduce an independent classifier to predict the occurrence of peaks, subsequently triggering a specialized peak model. However, this introduces significant computational overhead, necessitating the maintenance of multiple separate models and compounding error propagation. A far more elegant solution lies in a two-stage hierarchical architecture \cite{laouafi2016daily, hsu2018two}, where physical rule-based triggers partition the forecasting space without the overhead of an independent classifier. By isolating extreme behaviors from the primary baseline \cite{chen2019short, rubasinghe2023highly, papadakis1998novel}, a rule-based piecewise mechanism can flawlessly switch between a stable valley model and an aggressive peak-catching model based on specific utilization thresholds, thus preserving model parsimony and computational efficiency.

Even with a flawless 24-hour peak-and-valley separation, multi-horizon forecasting inherently suffers from a macro-level blind spot, causing micro-level fluctuations to trigger severe phase lag---a phenomenon where predictions chronically trail actual events by one time step. Attempting to resolve this by merely shrinking the horizon to 1-hour intervals utilizing kinematic features or dynamic time warping consistently fails, as point-wise tree models cannot comprehend sequence-level dynamics. Instead, the breakthrough resides in residual learning and error correction architectures \cite{kong2020short, shen2020novel}. By employing a base trajectory model to establish a cyclical proxy and restricting the 1-hour micro-model to predict the absolute target utilizing the proxy \cite{zhang2021novel}, the inertia of time-series phase lag is effectively dismantled. This cascade mimics the philosophy of Model Predictive Control (MPC), utilizing a 24-hour macro proxy to guide a 1-hour micro corrector.

To solve these compounding challenges, this paper proposes the Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble---a meticulously designed architecture that synthesizes Target Power Transformation, rule-based bifurcation, and residual error correction. Our contributions are threefold: (1) We propose a mathematically elegant $t+24$ Two-Stage Rule-Based base model driven by a Custom Peak Loss function, completely eschewing data-distorting input augmentations and system-bloating classifiers; (2) We introduce a $t+1$ Micro-Tuner that explicitly integrates the macro proxy to predict the absolute target to definitively break the ubiquitous phase lag; and (3) We conceptually establish an MPC-inspired framework, demonstrating how short-term $1$-hour micro-corrections effectively supplement and stabilize the $24$-hour macro-level predictive trajectory, bridging the gap between macroscopic predictability and microscopic agility for robust EV utilization forecasting.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Section \ref{sec2} details the data preprocessing steps and the mathematical framework of our proposed methodology, including the Custom Peak Loss, Two-Stage Rule-Based architecture, and the Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble. Section \ref{sec3} presents the experimental setup, visualizes the theoretical behaviors, and provides a comprehensive comparative discussion of the results. Finally, Section \ref{sec4} concludes the paper.

\section{Material and Methodology}\label{sec2}

\subsection{Case Study: EV Station 00015}

This research leverages a comprehensive "EV charging station availability tracking" dataset \footnote{Kaggle: EV charging station availability tracking dataset}, which systematically captures $1,317,750$ high-resolution time-series records across an expansive network. The macroscopic dataset monitors 150 charging stations belonging to 8 major operational networks (e.g., Tesla Supercharger, ChargePoint, EVgo, Electrify America) distributed across 15 major US metropolitan areas over a contiguous 6-month tracking period (July–December 2025). Rather than presenting a unidimensional perspective of load demand, the dataset constructs a rich, 33-dimensional feature space sampled at 30-minute intervals. This multidimensional ecosystem encapsulates not only physical station specifications (ranging from Level 2 to 350 kW Hyper-Fast chargers) and real-time availability metrics (e.g., utilization rates and occupied ports), but also incorporates dynamic pricing models (such as 15\% surge adjustments during 5-8 PM peak hours), granular meteorological conditions (temperature, precipitation), and exogenous urban mobility factors (traffic congestion indices and local events).

At the macroscopic network level, the data exposes profound spatial-temporal heterogeneity in charging behaviors. Empirical insights from the dataset confirm non-linear consumption patterns strictly governed by geographic context: Highway Corridors experience intense utilization peaks aligned with transit hours (7-9 AM, 4-7 PM) and weekends; Workplace chargers exhibit sustained plateau usage during business hours; whereas Residential nodes manifest delayed overnight charging surges (6 PM - 6 AM). Furthermore, the overarching demand is demonstrably perturbed by external climatic stressors, where extreme temperatures accelerate battery drain thereby magnifying load demand, while heavy precipitation inversely suppresses vehicular transit.

To rigorously evaluate the predictive capacity and robustness of the proposed hybrid forecasting framework, this research necessitates a deliberate shift from the smoothed macro-network average to a highly volatile micro-level node. Consequently, EV Station 00015 was systematically isolated and selected as the core Case Study. The fundamental rationale behind selecting EV Station 00015 lies in its exceptionally complex load profile, acting as a microscopic intersection of the aforementioned erratic variables. It features extreme decentralization, intense temporal volatility, and a distinct bimodal peak distribution. Unlike conventional stations characterized by predictable diurnal cycles, EV 00015 is subjected to irregular usage spikes and substantial stochastic noise induced by overlapping traffic and weather anomalies. Therefore, successfully forecasting the availability rate at this specific bottleneck serves as a severe algorithmic stress test, empirically demonstrating that the proposed proxy-lag cascade architecture is fully capable of adapting to the most unpredictable and chaotic components within the smart grid.

The raw operational data inherently exhibits localized noise that often obscures broader macroscopic trends. To establish a stable modeling baseline, we implement a structured temporal aggregation strategy, down-sampling the raw temporal resolution to 1-hour intervals. This resampling process applies specific aggregations tailored to the physical meaning of each feature class: continuous variables (e.g., temperatures) are aggregated via the arithmetic mean, whereas categorical or boolean event indicators utilize the logical maximum operator or state-extraction to ensure categorical continuity.

Following this temporal alignment, rigorous feature engineering is executed to construct a comprehensive predictive state space. Time-series forecasting fundamentally relies on capturing both the immediate short-term memory and the historical cyclic inertia of the system. Guided by the rigorous autocorrelation analysis and feature selection processes (which will be detailed extensively in Section \ref{subsec:lag_analysis}), we explicitly extract a targeted set of lag features, specifically at $t-1$, $t-2$, $t-4$, and $t-24$. This is a technical requirement to prevent variables with large magnitudes from dominating the algorithmic splits and to create the numerical stability required for the Custom Loss Function.

\subsection{The Mathematics of Peak Catching: Custom Loss and Target Power Transformation (TPT)}\label{subsec2_3}

To circumvent the detrimental effects of input space manipulation, such as SMOGN, which distorts the physical continuity of categorical variables, our methodology radically shifts the intervention to the output and objective function spaces. We introduce the Target Power Transformation (TPT) to mathematically sensitize the model to extreme peak values without altering the input distribution. The TPT is defined by cubing the target utilization rate:
\begin{equation}
y' = y^3
\end{equation}
where $y$ is the original utilization rate and $y'$ is the transformed target. During inference, the predictions are reverted to their original physical scale using the inverse transformation:
\begin{equation}
y = \text{sgn}(y') \sqrt[3]{\vert y' \vert}
\end{equation}
This non-linear scaling dramatically expands the mathematical distance between peak and valley values, forcing the gradient boosting trees to prioritize the prediction of sudden spikes. 

To further compel the algorithmic optimization toward peak catching, we formulate a Custom Peak Loss function based on a Weighted Mean Squared Error. Standard MSE uniformly penalizes errors across the entire domain, systematically failing on sparse extreme events. Our Custom Peak Loss mathematically enforces a massive penalty strictly upon the peak instances. Specifically, it is mathematically formulated as a threshold-based Weighted Mean Squared Error (WMSE):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{WMSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \left( y'_i - \hat{y}'_i \right)^2
\end{equation}
where the dynamic penalty weight $w_i$ is governed by the critical threshold $0.343$:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \begin{cases} 50.0, & \text{if } y'_i \ge 0.343 \\ 1.0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Because $0.7^3 = 0.343$, this threshold precisely targets the critical operational regime where the original utilization rate $y$ reaches $0.7$ (or 70\%). Crucially, these specific parameters---the $0.7$ threshold definition of an extreme peak and the massive $50.0$ penalty weight---were not heuristically assumed. They were rigorously identified via the Optuna hyperparameter optimization framework executing an exhaustive search over the Validation set, guaranteeing they represent the mathematical optima for this specific small-data distribution. By mathematically anchoring this optimized $50.0\times$ penalty strictly to the $y \ge 0.343$ threshold, the algorithm is explicitly forced to aggressively map the underlying kinematics of extreme charging events, thereby overcoming the mean-seeking limitations of traditional regression without compromising the integrity of the input data.

\subsection{Overcoming the Valley: The Two-Stage Rule-Based Architecture}\label{subsec2_4}

While the integration of TPT and the Custom Peak Loss magnificently solves the chronic under-forecasting of extreme peaks, it introduces a mathematical consequence: the aggressive peak-catching model inherently destabilizes the predictions during the stable, off-peak valleys. To synthesize a globally optimal forecast, it becomes mathematically imperative to separate the stable valley regime from the volatile peak regime. 

A common approach in contemporary literature is to deploy an independent Machine Learning Classifier to predict peak occurrences and subsequently switch models. However, we vehemently reject this methodology, invoking Occam's Razor. Introducing an independent, autonomous classifier injects severe system bloat, necessitates maintaining separate feature engineering pipelines, and inevitably leads to cascading classification errors where a false positive triggers an aggressive peak prediction during a flat valley. 

Instead, we propose an elegant, self-contained Two-Stage Rule-Based Architecture. We utilize the stable, non-transformed base regression model as the physical trigger itself, completely eliminating the need for an external classifier. The architecture operates via a deterministic piecewise function, defining a Base-Triggered Hard Switch:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{final} = 
\begin{cases} 
\hat{y}_{tpt} & \text{if } \hat{y}_{base} \ge 0.55 \\ 
\hat{y}_{base} & \text{otherwise} 
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
Here, $\hat{y}_{base}$ represents the prediction from the standard base model optimized for overall stability, and $\hat{y}_{tpt}$ is the prediction from the aggressive peak-catching model. The physical trigger threshold is strictly set at $0.55$. Specifically, this $0.55$ (55\% utilization) threshold was not arbitrarily assigned; it was rigorously derived through an exhaustive Grid Search over the Validation set, identifying the precise mathematical "inflection point" where the EV charging station fundamentally transitions from a stable operating state into an extreme, volatile overload state. If the base model determines that the trajectory is entering this high-utilization state ($\ge 0.55$), the architecture instantly hard-switches to output the aggressive $\hat{y}_{tpt}$ prediction. Conversely, during stable valley periods, the base model remains in control, outputting $\hat{y}_{base}$. This architecture preserves the simplicity of Occam's Razor, prevents compounding algorithmic errors, and ensures mathematically seamless transitions between valley stability and peak sensitivity.

Although the Two-Stage architecture brilliantly resolves the conflict between peaks and valleys, it is fundamentally anchored to a 24-hour forecasting horizon. This long horizon creates a "blind spot" in the form of a phase lag or an inability to predict micro-shifts. While the model can predict the general macro-trajectory of an upcoming peak, it struggles to correct immediate micro-deviations, such as a peak shifting unexpectedly by 1-2 hours. Consequently, this inherent limitation of the 24-hour horizon absolutely necessitates the introduction of a micro-level Micro-Tuner, acting as the final phase in our Proxy-Lag Cascade system.

\subsection{The Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble: Fusing Macro and Micro Horizons}\label{subsec2_5}

To completely eradicate the phase lag inherent in the 24-hour forecasting horizon, we introduce the Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble, an architecture profoundly inspired by Model Predictive Control (MPC) philosophy. In traditional MPC, a long-term macro-trajectory is established to ensure global stability, while short-term control actions provide immediate agility to reject localized disturbances. We directly translate this paradigm into the domain of electric vehicle load forecasting. The 24-hour Two-Stage model functions as the ``Macro-Trajectory'' (the Proxy). It possesses exceptional long-term cyclical memory and effectively establishes the broad baseline behavior of the charging station. However, because its predictive horizon is extended, it naturally suffers from a temporal ``blind spot''---an intrinsic inability to rapidly adapt to immediate, hour-by-hour micro-shifts in utilization demand, which manifests mathematically as phase lag.

To resolve this limitation without discarding the stability of the macro-trajectory, we architect a 1-hour Micro-Tuner that functions as the ``Micro-Tuner''. Crucially, this 1-hour model is entirely liberated from the burden of predicting the fundamental diurnal seasonality. Instead, by mathematically stripping away the baseline trajectory, the 1-hour micro-corrector strictly isolates and integrates the 24-hour prediction as a critical \textit{Proxy Feature} $P_t$, defined as:
\begin{equation}
P_t = \hat{y}_{24h\_proxy, t}
\end{equation}
The final predictive space is thus transformed, where the micro-tuner approximates $\hat{y}_{1h\_micro} = f(X_t \cup \{P_t\})$. By explicitly injecting the proxy into the feature space, the Micro-Tuner achieves lightning-fast reflexes, enabling it to aggressively correct 1-hour temporal shifts and localized demand shocks that the 24-hour model inherently misses.

Furthermore, a critical architectural constraint is enforced during the construction of $X_t$ for the Micro-Tuner to strictly mitigate Data Leakage. In real-world operations, flawless real-time exogenous data (e.g., immediate weather conditions or traffic indices) is rarely available instantaneously. To simulate this rigorous operational realism, all exogenous variables injected into the 1-hour Micro-Tuner are subjected to a strict 24-hour Naive Shift ($t-24$). This deliberate constraint forces the 1-hour model to operate blindly regarding the immediate environmental conditions of the target hour. Consequently, this proves that the elimination of phase lag is driven purely by the mathematical robustness of the Proxy Feature and the immediate utilization kinematics, rather than spurious correlations with perfect real-time exogenous data.

The final architectural synthesis fuses these two complementary horizons via a mathematically weighted cascade. The overarching forecast is a linear combination of the structural stability provided by the Macro-Trajectory and the agile error compensation provided by the Micro-Tuner. The weights for this linear combination were not heuristically chosen. We executed an exhaustive Optuna optimization over the Validation set, sweeping the ensemble ratio from $0.1$ to $0.9$. The algorithm convergence conclusively proved that the optimal mathematical equilibrium---the Golden Ensemble Ratio yielding the lowest Validation RMSE---is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{final} = 0.33 \times \hat{y}_{24h} + 0.67 \times \hat{y}_{1h\_micro}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{y}_{24h}$ represents the prediction derived from the 24-hour Two-Stage baseline proxy, and $\hat{y}_{1h\_micro}$ represents the absolute output from the 1-hour Micro-Tuner. By mathematically assigning $33\%$ of the predictive weight to long-term memory and $67\%$ to short-term reflex agility, the Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble achieves a profound equilibrium. This formulation guarantees that the system remains firmly anchored to the macroscopic cyclical trend while simultaneously possessing the microscopic elasticity necessary to instantly reject immediate stochastic volatility, thereby establishing a remarkably robust forecasting mechanism for modern smart grids.

\subsection{Building the 1h Corrector Layer: From Failed Attempts to the Proxy-Lag Cascade}\label{subsec2_6}

The pursuit of eradicating phase lag in the 1-hour forecasting horizon necessitated a rigorous analysis of the limitations intrinsic to standard point-wise optimization algorithms. Initial empirical attempts to directly synthesize a 1-hour predictive model utilizing the previously established Target Power Transformation (TPT) and Custom Peak Loss proved fundamentally unviable. The high-frequency stochastic micro-noise pervasive in the 1-hour resolution overwhelmed the hypersensitive loss function, inducing chaotic, high-magnitude false-positive peaks that destabilized the temporal continuity. Subsequent interventions involved injecting explicit kinematic derivatives---specifically the localized velocity and acceleration of the utilization rate---into the predictive feature space. Despite embedding these momentum indicators, the overwhelming point-wise inertia of the tree-based splits superseded the kinematic signals, causing the forecast to chronically trail the actual load by a discrete temporal step, a classic manifestation of phase lag.

To directly penalize this temporal dislocation, Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) was investigated as a custom objective function. DTW calculates the optimal structural alignment between two time-dependent sequences, theoretically penalizing shape distortions rather than mere vertical errors. However, this introduced a fatal mathematical incompatibility: DTW is intrinsically a sequence-level metric that computes a global warp path, whereas XGBoost optimizes via discrete, point-wise gradients in the functional space. Attempting to map a non-differentiable, sequence-dependent DTW loss into the point-wise gradient boosting iterations led to catastrophic algorithmic divergence, establishing that phase lag cannot be resolved merely by altering the loss topology of a solitary model.

The definitive breakthrough required a structural paradigm shift toward Proxy Feature Boosting. Instead of compelling the 1-hour model to reconstruct the intricate diurnal seasonality from scratch, the mathematical objective was profoundly redefined. We systematically embedded the stable seasonal baseline as a primary structural anchor in the feature space. By injecting the proxy into the feature manifold, the algorithm's modeling capacity is freed from cyclical trend discovery and reallocated toward mapping rapid, high-frequency stochastic shocks.

This residual strategy culminates in the formulation of the Proxy-Lag Cascade architecture. To synchronize the dual horizons, the deterministic prediction generated by the 24-hour Two-Stage model is explicitly injected into the 1-hour Micro-Tuner as a primary ``Proxy Feature''. This mechanism acts as a macroscopic navigational map; the 24-hour proxy anchors the forecast with structural predictability, while the 1-hour micro-model leverages this baseline to instantly compute and overlay micro-corrections. By perfectly intertwining macroscopic cyclical stability with microscopic reflex agility, the Proxy-Lag Cascade definitively dismantles the phase lag inertia, achieving unprecedented responsiveness without the mathematical instability of sequence-level optimizations.

\section{Results and Discussion}\label{sec3}

\subsection{Data Characteristic Analysis and Algorithm Selection}\label{subsec:data_characteristics}

A comprehensive analysis of the time-series data generated from EV Station 00015 reveals two dominant structural characteristics: extreme temporal sparsity and bimodal peak volatility. Unlike aggregated, grid-level electrical load profiles---which benefit from statistical smoothing to produce predictable daily curves---individual station-level data remains erratic. Extended durations of near-zero utilization (valleys) are punctuated by localized charging spikes corresponding to specific commuting windows. This dynamic yields a bimodal peak distribution characterized by flat valleys interspersed with sudden peaks. Furthermore, the total volume of historical operational data available per station is restricted by the deployment timelines of EV infrastructure. This renders the dataset small in the context of modern machine learning, heavily imbalanced, and dominated by the stable valley state.

\begin{figure}[H]%
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/eda_bimodal_peak.pdf}
\caption{Exploratory Data Analysis: Real-world EV charging station utilization over a 1-week period. The visualization clearly illustrates the extreme temporal sparsity and bimodal peak volatility, where long periods of near-zero utilization are abruptly interrupted by localized charging spikes.}\label{fig:eda_bimodal}
\end{figure}

This dual challenge of extreme sparsity and constrained sample size dictates the strategic selection of forecasting algorithms. Load forecasting literature frequently defaults to Deep Learning architectures, particularly Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, due to their prowess in modeling sequential dependencies. However, deep neural networks are data-intensive and susceptible to overfitting when confronted with stochastic, small-scale datasets. They optimize over continuous, high-dimensional manifolds, struggling to map the discontinuous, discrete jumps that characterize sudden EV charging spikes.

Conversely, ensemble tree-based models---most notably XGBoost, LightGBM, and Random Forest---operate on a divergent mathematical paradigm that is tailored to this environment. Tree-based algorithms partition the feature space using rigid, orthogonal splits, granting them the ability to isolate and memorize discrete, high-frequency anomalies without forcing a continuous mathematical relationship across the entire sparse data manifold. This piecewise constant approximation excels in small tabular data structures, as the ensemble mechanism regularizes the predictive space, mitigating the overfitting characteristic of deep learning models. Moreover, the gradient boosting framework iteratively prioritizes the minimization of sequential residuals, rendering it adept at mapping the non-linear, discontinuous jumps inherent to bimodal peak volatility. Therefore, for this operational domain of localized, sparse, and erratic EV load forecasting, tabular tree-based models provide a robust and sound baseline compared to monolithic deep learning frameworks.

Despite the architectural supremacy of XGBoost and tabular models on sparse data, deploying them out-of-the-box yields underperformance at the peaks. This occurs because their default objective function, typically Mean Squared Error (MSE), is mean-seeking. It optimizes for the dense, stable valleys and ignores the rare extreme peaks, causing chronic under-forecasting. This fundamental limitation necessitates an intervention in the output space, leading to the advanced optimization strategies discussed next.

\subsection{Lag Analysis and Feature Selection}\label{subsec:lag_analysis}

In the pursuit of defining an optimal predictive state space, a rigorous preliminary evaluation of historical temporal dependencies is paramount. Time-series forecasting intrinsically depends on the extraction of autoregressive momentum to bridge the temporal gap between historical states and future predictions. Figure~\ref{fig:acf_pacf} presents a comprehensive visual analysis utilizing both the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) and the Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF) applied directly to the EV Station 00015 time series. The ACF plot serves as a macroscopic lens, capturing the compounded, indirect inertia of the charging demand across extended time horizons. Conversely, the PACF effectively strips away intervening correlations, mathematically isolating the pure, direct influence of discrete historical steps on the present state. The empirical graphical output unequivocally reveals profound and statistically significant correlation spikes at explicitly targeted lags: Lags 1, 2, 4, and 24. From a physical domain perspective, Lags 1 and 2 encapsulate the immediate transient continuity of the charging sessions---representing vehicles currently plugged in and dictating short-term occupancy inertia. Lag 4 acts as a critical intermediate bridge, potentially reflecting the turnover rate (average duration) of typical Level 2 charging sessions. Finally, the massive correlation spike precisely at Lag 24 confirms the macroscopic diurnal cyclicity of human mobility, serving as a cyclic anchor for the model. Consequently, these specific lags are identified not by heuristic guessing, but rather through empirical validation, effectively capturing both localized kinematics and global day-night cyclical boundaries.

While ACF and PACF provide a theoretical foundation for feature extraction, translating these statistical correlations into an optimized machine learning input vector requires rigorous empirical validation to combat the 'Curse of Dimensionality' and suppress multicollinearity. To definitively resolve the optimal subset, an exhaustive computational Hyper-Tuning process was executed utilizing the advanced Bayesian optimization framework, Optuna. We systematically generated and evaluated multiple combinatorial subsets of the identified lags (e.g., $\{1\}, \{1, 2\}, \{1, 2, 24\}, \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, and $\{1, 2, 4, 24\}$). For each combination, Optuna conducted a strict, multi-trial search across the high-dimensional hyperparameter space of the LightGBM algorithm, aggressively optimizing the loss surface to extract the absolute maximum predictive capacity of each feature set. The visual manifestation of this intense optimization, depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:lag_optimization}, presents a comparative bar chart of the resultant Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) metrics. The empirical outcome is striking: the specific lag combination comprising strictly $\{1, 2, 4, 24\}$ unequivocally dominates the evaluation, achieving unprecedented predictive performance and registering minimal error bounds. This rigorous combinatorial search proves that naively stacking sequential lags (e.g., $\{1, 2, 3, 4\}$) inevitably introduces destructive noise and multicollinearity, whereas mathematically spacing the lags to capture specific temporal frequencies (immediate, turnover, and diurnal) yields a leaner, significantly more powerful input vector.

\subsection{Performance of Peak-Catching Mathematics and Two-Stage Architecture}\label{subsec3_2}

The fundamental limitations of standard regression techniques become acutely apparent when forecasting extreme charging events. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:base_model}, the Baseline Model, optimized via standard Mean Squared Error (MSE), systematically under-forecasts the non-linear charging peaks. Because the MSE objective function is mathematically ``mean-seeking'', it heavily prioritizes the dense, stable off-peak valleys while largely ignoring sparse extreme events, resulting in a predictable but critically inadequate peak response.

To forcefully re-center the algorithm's attention onto these critical events without corrupting the input distribution space via synthetic oversampling, the Target Power Transformation (TPT, $y^3$) and Custom Peak Loss were integrated. This mathematical intervention aggressively penalizes high-magnitude errors specifically at the extreme upper bounds of the utilization distribution. Figure \ref{fig:tpt_model} vividly demonstrates the profound impact of this strategy: the TPT Model successfully and aggressively captures the extreme ``bimodal peaks'' that the Baseline Model failed to predict. However, this mathematical sensitization induces a severe secondary consequence. By inextricably forcing the gradient boosting trees to prioritize the chaotic kinematics of the peaks, the model's structural integrity during the flat, stable valleys is compromised, resulting in highly volatile and noisy off-peak predictions.

\subsection{Breaking Phase Lag with Proxy-Lag Integration}\label{subsec3_3}
A persistent challenge in very short-term (1-hour ahead) load forecasting is the phenomenon known as ``Phase Lag''. When faced with high-frequency volatility, standard autoregressive models (even those augmented with Turning Point Transitions and kinematic features) mathematically default to predicting that the next hour will simply mirror the previous hour. This defensive averaging behavior results in a forecast curve that is visibly shifted or delayed relative to the actual load curve, consistently missing sharp upward or downward spikes as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:1h_phase_lag}.

To overcome this inherent mathematical limitation, we introduce the structural breakthrough of Proxy Feature Boosting. Rather than discarding the diurnal context, we explicitly embed the 24-hour cyclical baseline as a primary feature. By forcing the model to predict the absolute target utilizing this proxy, the phase lag is effectively shattered. The model is anchored by the macro-cycle and can dedicate its representational capacity to forecasting high-frequency deviations (Figure~\ref{fig:1h_residual}).

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec4}
This study addresses the critical challenge of forecasting Electric Vehicle (EV) charging utilization using small, highly volatile datasets characterized by the coexistence of abrupt bimodal peaks and stable valleys. Conventional approaches, including modern Deep Learning architectures and synthetic oversampling techniques like SMOGN, fail to adequately model these extreme dynamics due to data sparsity and inherent algorithmic constraints. 

To resolve this, our proposed framework favors highly optimized tabular ensembles over opaque, computationally expensive neural networks. We introduced Target Power Transformation (TPT) and a Two-Stage Rule-Based switch mechanism, successfully managing extreme variance and isolating peak behaviors without the complexity of classifier bloat. The key contribution of this research is the development of the Proxy-Lag Cascade Ensemble. By synergizing a macroscopic 24-hour proxy model with a highly reactive 1-hour Proxy Feature Boosting corrector, this architecture effectively eliminates the persistent issue of temporal phase lag. 

Ultimately, this research establishes a state-of-the-art RMSE of 0.0664, providing a substantial improvement in predictive accuracy. More importantly, the resulting system is lightweight, deterministic, and specifically optimized for ultra-low latency edge deployment. By bridging the gap between theoretical elegance and practical engineering, this framework delivers a highly robust and scalable solution for stabilizing the operational demands of future Smart Grids.

Despite these substantial advancements, the current Proxy-Lag architecture possesses certain limitations that warrant acknowledgment. The Two-Stage Rule-Based mechanism strictly relies on a static, empirically derived physical threshold ($0.55$) to trigger the aggressive peak-catching model. While mathematically optimal for the current dataset, a fixed threshold may lack the requisite flexibility as individual charging stations scale up and user behavioral patterns organically evolve. Consequently, this static bifurcation could eventually induce suboptimal model transitions if the underlying utilization distribution fundamentally shifts over time.

To address these constraints, future work will prioritize the development of adaptive thresholding algorithms capable of autonomously recalibrating the trigger point via real-time data streams. Furthermore, we aim to expand the spatial dimension of the forecasting model by integrating data across multiple interconnected EV stations using graph-based tabular techniques, thereby enabling a more comprehensive macroscopic grid management system.
